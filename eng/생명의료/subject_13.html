KAIST RESEARCH ACHIEVEMENTS

OncoSearch: A web tool
that searches biomedical literature for meaningful
pairs of cancers and genes

School of Computing Jong C. Park

Summary

Big data-enabled OncoSearch will be critically useful to experts in medicine, biology and bioinformatics in the world through a highly available and reliable web interface. Any researcher can use the data and the associated tools without any restriction to their research on complex diseases to efficiently validate large-scale experiments and to deepen the understanding of disease development mechanisms. In addition, OncoSearch will be used to discover new biomedical knowledge on complex diseases with the help of an automatic gene network inference system.

R&D Report

For cancer research, it is essential to identify various genes that are involved in oncogenesis and to understand how the genes affect cancers. Large-scale and individual genetic studies have been devoted to this field of research and massive amounts of cancer genetic data have been accumulating rapidly. Thus there is a strong need to review and integrate multi-dimensional data in a timely and effective manner. A big data platform-based search engine with advanced text-mining techniques has become invaluable.

OncoSearch is a web-based engine that searches Medline abstracts for sentences that mention gene expression changes in cancers, with queries that specify (i) whether a gene expression level is up-regulated or down-regulated, (ii) whether a certain type of cancer progresses or regresses along with such gene expression change and (iii) the expected role of the gene in the cancer. OncoSearch is the first of its kind that effectively extracts and infers the gene's class from existing biomedical literature. The three gene classes are oncogene(a gene that causes cells to acquire cancerous properties), tumor suppressor gene (a gene that causes cells to lose cancerous properties) and biomarker(a gene that is not identified as an oncogene or a tumor suppressor gene). This field of research is apparently in its early stages and the definitions of terms as used in biology databases are different from one another, given the fact that only 6.87% of the oncogenes and 3.76% of the tumor suppressor genes in UniProtKB(one of the de facto standard gene databases) are designated as such in the Vogelstein list.
